● [0:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sWUyFQQ5QeI&t=0). Introduction to polar coordinates.
     ◉ Goal:
          ○ represent curves using angles and distances instead of rectangular coordinates
     ◉ Main idea:
          ○ polar graph paper uses concentric circles to measure distance from a central point
     ◉ Reason:
          ○ some curves are easier, nicer, or more relevant to describe without $𝒙$ and $𝒚$
     ◉ Important:
          ○ using polar graph paper helps visualize the angle and the directed distance directly


● [1:25](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sWUyFQQ5QeI&t=85). Definition — The polar coordinate system.
     ◉ Polar axis:
          ○ the initial ray from which the angle starts
          ○ it plays the role of the positive x-axis
     ◉ Pole:
          ○ the central fixed point
          ○ this is the origin
     ◉ Main idea:
          ○ a point is described by an ordered pair $(𝒓,𝜽)$
     ◉ Meaning:
          ○ $𝜽$ gives the angle from the polar axis
          ○ $𝒓$ gives the directed distance from the pole to the point


● [4:17](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sWUyFQQ5QeI&t=257). Orientation of the angle $𝜽$.
     ◉ Definition:
          ○ $𝜽$ measures angular movement from the polar axis
     ◉ i̲f̲ $𝜽>0$
          ○ t̲h̲e̲n̲ the direction is counterclockwise
     ◉ i̲f̲ $𝜽<0$
          ○ t̲h̲e̲n̲ the direction is clockwise
     ◉ Important:
          ○ the sign of the angle determines the direction of rotation


● [4:53](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sWUyFQQ5QeI&t=293). The directed distance $𝒓$.
     ◉ Definition:
          ○ $𝒓$ is the distance from the pole to the point
     ◉ i̲f̲ $𝒓>0$
          ○ t̲h̲e̲n̲ the point lies on the terminal side of the angle
     ◉ i̲f̲ $𝒓<0$
          ○ t̲h̲e̲n̲ use the given angle first
          ○ then reflect the point $180^\circ$ through the pole
     ◉ Meaning:
          ○ negative $𝒓$ sends the point in the opposite direction along the same line

● [16:53](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sWUyFQQ5QeI&t=1013). Coordinate conversion between polar and rectangular systems.
     ◉ Goal:
          ○ move back and forth between $(𝒓,𝜽)$ and $(𝒙,𝒚)$
     ◉ Main idea:
          ○ use right-triangle trigonometry
     ◉ Polar to rectangular:
          ○ $𝒙=𝒓\cdot \cos(𝜽)$
          ○ $𝒚=𝒓\cdot \sin(𝜽)$
     ◉ Rectangular to polar:
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $𝒓^{2}=𝒙^{2}+𝒚^{2}$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $\tan(𝜽)=\dfrac{𝒚}{𝒙}$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ Important:
          ○ always graph the point first
          ○ this prevents quadrant mistakes when choosing the angle
     ◉ Warning:
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ the square root in $𝒓=\sqrt{𝒙^{2}+𝒚^{2}}$ does not by itself fix the correct quadrant
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ the quadrant must be handled through the angle $𝜽$

● [27:39](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sWUyFQQ5QeI&t=1659). 🧩 Conversion — Rectangular to polar: $(-1,1)$. [📷image](../img/Calculus 2 Lecture 10.4/[27-39]-01.png)

          ○ $𝒓^{2}=(-1)^{2}+1^{2}=2$

          ○ the reference angle is $\dfrac{\pi}{4}$

               $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ Final result:

          $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ Important:
          ○ do not choose $-\dfrac{\pi}{4}$ here

● [33:43](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sWUyFQQ5QeI&t=2023). 🧩 Conversion — Rectangular to polar: $(3,-4)$. [📷image](../img/Calculus 2 Lecture 10.4/[33-43]-01.png)

     ◉ Find $𝒓$.

          ○ $𝒓^{2}=3^{2}+(-4)^{2}=25$

● [39:06](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sWUyFQQ5QeI&t=2346). 🧩 Convert the polar equation $𝒓=2\cdot \sin(𝜽)$ to rectangular form. [📷image](../img/Calculus 2 Lecture 10.4/[39-06]-01.png)
     ◉ Goal:
          ○ identify the curve in rectangular coordinates
     ◉ Main idea:
          ○ use $𝒓^{2}=𝒙^{2}+𝒚^{2}$ as the bridge between systems
     ◉ Procedure:
          ○ multiply both sides by $𝒓$:
               ■ $𝒓^{2}=2𝒓\cdot \sin(𝜽)$
          ○ substitute:
               ■ $𝒓^{2}=𝒙^{2}+𝒚^{2}$
               ■ $𝒓\cdot \sin(𝜽)=𝒚$
          ○ therefore:
               ■ $𝒙^{2}+𝒚^{2}=2𝒚$
     ◉ Rewrite:
          ○ $𝒙^{2}+𝒚^{2}-2𝒚=0$
     ◉ Complete the square:
          ○ $𝒙^{2}+𝒚^{2}-2𝒚+1=1$
          ○ $𝒙^{2}+(𝒚-1)^{2}=1$
     ◉ Final result:
          ○ the curve is a circle
     ◉ Final interpretation:
          ○ center = $(0,1)$
          ○ radius = $1$

     ◉ Symmetry about the polar axis:

● [48:45](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sWUyFQQ5QeI&t=2925). 🧩 Graph the polar equation $𝒓=1+\cos(𝜽)$. [📷image](../img/Calculus 2 Lecture 10.4/[48-45]-01.png)
     ◉ ❶ First check what kind of equation it is.
          ○ this is already a polar equation
          ○ it uses only $𝒓$ and $𝜽$
     ◉ ❷ Test symmetry.
          ○ let $𝒇(𝜽)=1+\cos(𝜽)$
          ○ $𝒇(-𝜽)=1+\cos(-𝜽)$
          ○ $𝒇(-𝜽)=1+\cos(𝜽)$
          ○ therefore:
               ■ $𝒇(-𝜽)=𝒇(𝜽)$
               ■ the graph is symmetric about the polar axis
     ◉ ❸ Use the symmetry.
          ○ because the graph is symmetric about the polar axis,
               ■ it is enough to graph only $0\leq 𝜽\leq \pi$
               ■ then reflect across the polar axis
     ◉ ❹ Build a table of easy values.
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $𝜽=0$        →   $𝒓=2$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $𝜽=\dfrac{\pi}{4}$      →   $𝒓=1+\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$

     ◉ Final result:
          ○ the graph is a cardioid
     ◉ Final interpretation:

          ○ and symmetric about the polar axis
     ◉ N̲O̲T̲E̲ — General workflow for graphing polar equations.
          ○ ❶ First decide what kind of equation you have.

          ○ ❷ After that, test symmetry.

          ○ ❸ Once you find symmetry, use it.

          ○ ❹ Then make a table of convenient angles.

● [1:26:24](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sWUyFQQ5QeI&t=5184). 🧩 Example 5 — Sketch the curve $(𝒙^{2}+𝒚^{2})^{2}=4(𝒙^{2}-𝒚^{2})$. [📷image](../img/Calculus 2 Lecture 10.4/[1-26-24]-01.png)
     ◉ ❶ First identify what kind of equation it is.

          ○ so convert it to polar form first
     ◉ ❷ Use the standard conversions.
          ○ $𝒙^{2}+𝒚^{2}=𝒓^{2}$
          ○ $𝒙=𝒓\cdot \cos(𝜽)$
          ○ $𝒚=𝒓\cdot \sin(𝜽)$
     ◉ ❸ Substitute into the equation.
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $(𝒓^{2})^{2}=4\left[(𝒓\cdot \cos(𝜽))^{2}-(𝒓\cdot \sin(𝜽))^{2}\right]$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $𝒓^{4}=4(𝒓^{2}\cos^{2}(𝜽)-𝒓^{2}\sin^{2}(𝜽))$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $𝒓^{4}=4𝒓^{2}(\cos^{2}(𝜽)-\sin^{2}(𝜽))$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ ❹ Use the identity:
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $\cos^{2}(𝜽)-\sin^{2}(𝜽)=\cos(2𝜽)$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ therefore:
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
               ■ $𝒓^{4}=4𝒓^{2}\cos(2𝜽)$
               $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ ❺ Divide by $𝒓^{2}$.
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $𝒓^{2}=4\cos(2𝜽)$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ ❻ Solve for $𝒓$.
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $𝒓=\sqrt{4\cos(2𝜽)}$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $𝒓=2\sqrt{\cos(2𝜽)}$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ ❼ Use symmetry.
          ○ from the original rectangular equation:
               ■ all the powers of $𝒙$ and $𝒚$ are even
               ■ so the graph is symmetric about the x-axis and the y-axis
          ○ in polar language, this means:
               ■ symmetric about the polar axis
               ■ symmetric about the line $𝜽=\dfrac{\pi}{2}$
     ◉ ❽ Check the domain.
          ○ because of the square root, we need:
               ■ $\cos(2𝜽)\geq 0$
          ○ therefore the graph is undefined when $\cos(2𝜽)<0$
     ◉ ❾ Plot only the part that is defined.
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ for $0\leq 𝜽\leq \dfrac{\pi}{4}$:
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
               ■ $𝜽=0$      →   $𝒓=2$
               $\rule{0pt}{}$
               ■ $𝜽=\dfrac{\pi}{8}$    →   $𝒓=2\sqrt{\cos\left(\dfrac{\pi}{4}\right)}=2\sqrt{\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}}$
               $\rule{0pt}{}$
               ■ $𝜽=\dfrac{\pi}{4}$    →   $𝒓=0$
               $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ but for larger angles such as $\dfrac{3\pi}{8}$ or $\dfrac{\pi}{2}$:
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
               ■ $\cos(2𝜽)$ becomes negative,
               ■ so $𝒓$ is undefined there
     ◉ ❿ Use symmetry to complete the graph.
          ○ first draw the defined arc from $𝜽=0$ to $𝜽=\dfrac{\pi}{4}$
          ○ reflect it across the polar axis
          ○ then reflect those two pieces across the line $𝜽=\dfrac{\pi}{2}$
     ◉ Final result:
          ○ the graph is a lemniscate
     ◉ Final interpretation:
          ○ the curve has the shape of an infinity sign
     ◉ N̲O̲T̲E̲ — Main idea of this example.
          ○ unlike the previous rose-curve example, here the square root creates a domain restriction
          ○ so we cannot keep plotting through all the usual sample angles
          ○ once the inside of the square root becomes negative, the graph stops there
          ○ then symmetry is used to generate the rest of the curve

● [1:37:31](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sWUyFQQ5QeI&t=5851). Tangent lines and slopes for polar curves.
     ◉ Goal:
          ○ find the slope $\dfrac{𝒅𝒚}{𝒅𝒙}$ for a curve given in polar form
     ◉ Main idea:
          ○ polar equations are not exactly parametric equations
          ○ but they behave like parametric equations with parameter $𝜽$
     ◉ Use the rectangular relations:
          ○ $𝒙=𝒓\cos(𝜽)$
          ○ $𝒚=𝒓\sin(𝜽)$
     ◉ Important:
          ○ here $𝒓$ is not a constant
          ○ $𝒓$ is a function of $𝜽$
          ○ so we must use the product rule
          ○ and treat $𝒓$ implicitly as depending on $𝜽$
     ◉ Meaning of the derivative:
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ even for a polar curve, $\dfrac{𝒅𝒚}{𝒅𝒙}$ still means the usual rectangular slope
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ that is, rise over run


● [1:39:23](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sWUyFQQ5QeI&t=5963). Polar curves as parametric curves.
     ◉ Since:
          ○ $𝒙=𝒙(𝜽)$
          ○ $𝒚=𝒚(𝜽)$
     ◉ Then:
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $\dfrac{𝒅𝒚}{𝒅𝒙}=\dfrac{\dfrac{𝒅𝒚}{𝒅𝜽}}{\dfrac{𝒅𝒙}{𝒅𝜽}}$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ This is the same idea as in parametric calculus,
          ○ only now the parameter is $𝜽$ instead of $𝒕$


● [1:40:03](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sWUyFQQ5QeI&t=6003). Deriving $\dfrac{𝒅𝒚}{𝒅𝜽}$.
     ◉ Start with:
          ○ $𝒚=𝒓\sin(𝜽)$
     ◉ Differentiate with respect to $𝜽$.
     ◉ Use the product rule:
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $\dfrac{𝒅𝒚}{𝒅𝜽}=\dfrac{𝒅}{𝒅𝜽}[𝒓]\cdot \sin(𝜽)+𝒓\cdot \dfrac{𝒅}{𝒅𝜽}[\sin(𝜽)]$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ Therefore:
          ○ $\dfrac{𝒅𝒚}{𝒅𝜽}=\left(\dfrac{𝒅𝒓}{𝒅𝜽}\right)\sin(𝜽)+𝒓\cos(𝜽)$


● [1:44:04](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sWUyFQQ5QeI&t=6244). Deriving $\dfrac{𝒅𝒙}{𝒅𝜽}$.
     ◉ Start with:
          ○ $𝒙=𝒓\cos(𝜽)$
     ◉ Differentiate with respect to $𝜽$.
     ◉ Use the product rule:
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $\dfrac{𝒅𝒙}{𝒅𝜽}=\dfrac{𝒅}{𝒅𝜽}[𝒓]\cdot \cos(𝜽)+𝒓\cdot \dfrac{𝒅}{𝒅𝜽}[\cos(𝜽)]$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ Therefore:
          ○ $\dfrac{𝒅𝒙}{𝒅𝜽}=\left(\dfrac{𝒅𝒓}{𝒅𝜽}\right)\cos(𝜽)-𝒓\sin(𝜽)$


● [1:45:19](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sWUyFQQ5QeI&t=6319). Final slope formula for polar curves.
     ◉ Substitute into the parametric-style ratio:
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $\dfrac{𝒅𝒚}{𝒅𝒙}=\dfrac{\dfrac{𝒅𝒚}{𝒅𝜽}}{\dfrac{𝒅𝒙}{𝒅𝜽}}$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ Final result:
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $\dfrac{𝒅𝒚}{𝒅𝒙}=\dfrac{\left(\dfrac{𝒅𝒓}{𝒅𝜽}\right)\sin(𝜽)+𝒓\cos(𝜽)}{\left(\dfrac{𝒅𝒓}{𝒅𝜽}\right)\cos(𝜽)-𝒓\sin(𝜽)}$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$

● [1:59:36](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sWUyFQQ5QeI&t=7176). Horizontal and vertical tangents. [📷image](../img/Calculus 2 Lecture 10.4/[1-59-36]-01.png)
     ◉ Horizontal tangents:
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ i̲f̲ $\dfrac{𝒅𝒚}{𝒅𝜽}=0$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
               ■ t̲h̲e̲n̲ the slope is zero
               ■ so the tangent is horizontal
     ◉ Vertical tangents:
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ i̲f̲ $\dfrac{𝒅𝒙}{𝒅𝜽}=0$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
               ■ t̲h̲e̲n̲ the slope is undefined
               ■ so the tangent is vertical
     ◉ In terms of the formulas:
          ○ horizontal tangent when
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
               ■ $\left(\dfrac{𝒅𝒓}{𝒅𝜽}\right)\sin(𝜽)+𝒓\cos(𝜽)=0$
               $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ vertical tangent when
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
               ■ $\left(\dfrac{𝒅𝒓}{𝒅𝜽}\right)\cos(𝜽)-𝒓\sin(𝜽)=0$


● N̲O̲T̲E̲ 1 — Why the product rule is needed.
     ◉ in $𝒙=𝒓\cos(𝜽)$ and $𝒚=𝒓\sin(𝜽)$,
          ○ both factors depend on $𝜽$
     ◉ $\cos(𝜽)$ and $\sin(𝜽)$ clearly depend on $𝜽$
     ◉ and $𝒓$ also depends on $𝜽$ because the polar equation has the form $𝒓=𝒇(𝜽)$
     ◉ so each derivative requires the product rule


● N̲O̲T̲E̲ 2 — Why $\dfrac{𝒅𝒓}{𝒅𝜽}$ appears.
$\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ $𝒓$ is not treated as a constant
     ◉ it changes as $𝜽$ changes
     ◉ so when differentiating $𝒓$ with respect to $𝜽$,
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ we get $\dfrac{𝒅𝒓}{𝒅𝜽}$


● N̲O̲T̲E̲ 3 — Main workflow for polar slope problems.
     ◉ ❶ Write:
          ○ $𝒙=𝒓\cos(𝜽)$
          ○ $𝒚=𝒓\sin(𝜽)$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ ❷ Find $\dfrac{𝒅𝒓}{𝒅𝜽}$.
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ ❸ Compute:
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $\dfrac{𝒅𝒚}{𝒅𝜽}=\left(\dfrac{𝒅𝒓}{𝒅𝜽}\right)\sin(𝜽)+𝒓\cos(𝜽)$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $\dfrac{𝒅𝒙}{𝒅𝜽}=\left(\dfrac{𝒅𝒓}{𝒅𝜽}\right)\cos(𝜽)-𝒓\sin(𝜽)$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ ❹ Form the ratio:
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $\dfrac{𝒅𝒚}{𝒅𝒙}=\dfrac{\dfrac{𝒅𝒚}{𝒅𝜽}}{\dfrac{𝒅𝒙}{𝒅𝜽}}$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ ❺ Then evaluate at the requested angle
          ○ or set the numerator or denominator equal to zero for tangent-line questions

● [1:46:50](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sWUyFQQ5QeI&t=6410). 🧩 Example 6 — Find $\dfrac{𝒅𝒚}{𝒅𝒙}$ and the slope of the tangent line for $𝒓=1+\cos(𝜽)$, at $𝜽=\dfrac{\pi}{6}$.
     ◉ ❶ Find $\dfrac{𝒅𝒓}{𝒅𝜽}$ first.
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $𝒓=1+\cos(𝜽)$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $\dfrac{𝒅𝒓}{𝒅𝜽}=-\sin(𝜽)$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ ❷ Use the polar slope formula.
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $\dfrac{𝒅𝒚}{𝒅𝒙}=\dfrac{\left(\dfrac{𝒅𝒓}{𝒅𝜽}\right)\sin(𝜽)+𝒓\cos(𝜽)}{\left(\dfrac{𝒅𝒓}{𝒅𝜽}\right)\cos(𝜽)-𝒓\sin(𝜽)}$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ ❸ Substitute $𝒓$ and $\dfrac{𝒅𝒓}{𝒅𝜽}$.
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $\dfrac{𝒅𝒚}{𝒅𝒙}=\dfrac{\left(-\sin(𝜽)\right)\sin(𝜽)+\left(1+\cos(𝜽)\right)\cos(𝜽)}{\left(-\sin(𝜽)\right)\cos(𝜽)-\left(1+\cos(𝜽)\right)\sin(𝜽)}$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ ❹ Expand and simplify.
          ○ numerator:
               ■ $-\sin^{2}(𝜽)+\cos(𝜽)+\cos^{2}(𝜽)$
               ■ $=\cos^{2}(𝜽)-\sin^{2}(𝜽)+\cos(𝜽)$
               ■ $=\cos(2𝜽)+\cos(𝜽)$
          ○ denominator:
               ■ $-\sin(𝜽)\cos(𝜽)-\sin(𝜽)-\sin(𝜽)\cos(𝜽)$
               ■ $=-2\sin(𝜽)\cos(𝜽)-\sin(𝜽)$
               ■ $=-\sin(2𝜽)-\sin(𝜽)$
     ◉ ❺ Therefore:
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $\dfrac{𝒅𝒚}{𝒅𝒙}=\dfrac{\cos(2𝜽)+\cos(𝜽)}{-\sin(2𝜽)-\sin(𝜽)}$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $\dfrac{𝒅𝒚}{𝒅𝒙}=-\dfrac{\cos(2𝜽)+\cos(𝜽)}{\sin(2𝜽)+\sin(𝜽)}$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ ❻ Evaluate at $𝜽=\dfrac{\pi}{6}$.
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $𝒎=-\dfrac{\cos\left(\dfrac{\pi}{3}\right)+\cos\left(\dfrac{\pi}{6}\right)}{\sin\left(\dfrac{\pi}{3}\right)+\sin\left(\dfrac{\pi}{6}\right)}$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $𝒎=-\dfrac{\dfrac{1}{2}+\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}}{\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}+\dfrac{1}{2}}$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $𝒎=-1$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ Final result:
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ $\dfrac{𝒅𝒚}{𝒅𝒙}=-\dfrac{\cos(2𝜽)+\cos(𝜽)}{\sin(2𝜽)+\sin(𝜽)}$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ at $𝜽=\dfrac{\pi}{6}$, $𝒎=-1$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ Final interpretation:
          ○ the tangent line to the cardioid at $𝜽=\dfrac{\pi}{6}$ has slope $-1$

● N̲O̲T̲E̲ 4 — Horizontal and vertical tangents for polar curves.
     ◉ horizontal tangents happen when the main numerator is zero
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ that is, when $\dfrac{𝒅𝒚}{𝒅𝜽}=0$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ vertical tangents happen when the main denominator is zero
     $\rule{0pt}{}$
          ○ that is, when $\dfrac{𝒅𝒙}{𝒅𝜽}=0$
          $\rule{0pt}{}$
     ◉ Main idea:
          ○ this is exactly analogous to the parametric case
          ○ only now the parameter is $𝜽$ instead of $𝒕$
